INTRODUCTION

Flow measurement deals with quantifying the rate at which a fluid moves through a system. Its applications include billing for supplied materials (e.g., fuel), ensuring efficient process control, and maintaining the desired flow rates. Various devices and methods exist, each designed to cater to different accuracy, repeatability, and cost requirements.

BASIC PRINCIPLES OF FLOW MEASUREMENT

Flow can be measured as either a volumetric quantity or a flow rate (often derived from velocity). Among other factors, fluid viscosity, density, and the internal geometry of pipes play key roles in determining accurate flow readings.

Streamlined and Turbulent Flow

At low velocities, fluid flow is laminar or streamlined: particles move in parallel lines with minimal mixing. Above a critical velocity, transverse components in fluid motion increase, causing turbulent flow.

The parameter defining flow regime is the Reynolds number (Re):

Re = ( D × v × ρ ) / μ

  • If Re < 2000, flow is laminar.
  • If Re > 4000, flow is turbulent.
  • The zone 2000 < Re < 4000 is critical and may transition between laminar and turbulent.

Flow Profile

The velocity of a fluid across a pipe’s cross-section can vary significantly. In laminar flow, the profile is parabolic, whereas in turbulent flow under fully developed conditions, the center velocity is about 1.2 times the mean velocity. Ensuring a fully developed flow profile is critical for repeatable and accurate measurement.

Energy of a Fluid in Motion

The energy components in any steady flow without friction can be expressed as:

Total Energy = Potential Energy + Kinetic Energy + Pressure Energy

Where each term relates to the fluid’s vertical position, velocity, and pressure respectively. Commonly in flow equations:

E = Z × g + V2 / (2 × g) + p / ρ

  • Z = height (elevation)
  • g = gravitational acceleration
  • V = velocity of the fluid
  • p = pressure
  • ρ = density of the fluid

Viscosity

Viscosity is the internal friction in a fluid that resists flow. Higher viscosity dampens disturbances and swirls, requiring longer straight runs to achieve reliable measurement conditions. For liquids, viscosity typically decreases with increasing temperature; for gases, it usually increases with temperature.

Bernoulli’s Theorem

Bernoulli’s theorem states that the sum of potential, kinetic, and pressure energy along a streamline (in steady, frictionless flow) is constant. The fundamental form of Bernoulli’s equation:

Z × g + (V2 / 2g) + (p / ρ) = constant

Where each variable represents the components of fluid energy at any specific location along the streamline.

Modification of Flow Equations for Gases

When gases compress or expand, Bernoulli’s equations are adjusted to include a factor that corrects for changes in density. An expansibility factor (ε) accounts for volume change due to compression or expansion:

Q = f(ε) × [Bernoulli-based terms]

ε is unity (1) for incompressible fluids but drops slightly below 1 for real gases.

FLUID FLOW IN CLOSED PIPES

In closed conduits (e.g., pipes) transporting liquids or gases under pressure, numerous techniques exist to measure flow. Each has unique benefits and constraints.

Differential-Pressure Devices

  • Principle: A constriction causes a pressure drop proportional to flow rate.
  • Examples: Orifice plates, venturi tubes, nozzles, and more.
  • Pros: Mature technology, widely used, cost-effective for many applications.
  • Cons: Creates permanent pressure loss, requires accurate calculation or calibration, and limited rangeability.

Orifice Plate

  • Principle: A thin plate with a precisely machined hole creates differential pressure.
  • Pros: Simple design and installation, inexpensive, reliable.
  • Cons: High permanent pressure loss; sensitive to wear or damage around the orifice.

Venturi Tube

  • Principle: Gradual constriction and expansion create a measurable pressure drop.
  • Pros: Low permanent pressure drop, reliable for large flows, often used in water & wastewater.
  • Cons: More expensive and bulkier than an orifice plate; installation space required.

Rotameter

  • Principle: Fluid flow lifts a float within a tapered tube, where height corresponds to flow rate.
  • Pros: Simple design, direct reading, good for low flow rates.
  • Cons: Limited to vertical orientation, must be transparent, works primarily for clean fluids.

Pitot Tube

  • Principle: Measures impact and static pressures to determine fluid velocity.
  • Pros: Economical, minimal flow obstruction, useful for velocity profiling.
  • Cons: Not ideal for very low or high flow rates; accuracy depends on alignment and calibration.

Ultrasonic Flow Meter

  • Principle: Uses the transit time or Doppler shift of ultrasound signals through the fluid to measure velocity.
  • Pros: Non-intrusive, low maintenance, can work for various fluids without major pressure loss.
  • Cons: Accuracy can be affected by pipe scale, very high turndown ratio can be challenging, requires good fluid acoustics.

Magnetic Flow Meter

  • Principle: Flowing conductive fluids generate a voltage across a magnetic field; the voltage is proportional to flow.
  • Pros: No moving parts, minimal pressure drop, excellent for slurries & corrosive fluids.
  • Cons: Only works with conductive fluids; more expensive than some alternatives.
